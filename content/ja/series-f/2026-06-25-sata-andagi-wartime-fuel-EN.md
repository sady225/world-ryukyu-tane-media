# Okinawa's Deep-Fried Donut and the Ghost of Wartime Fuel

*A conversation with the last witnesses of postwar Okinawa*

---

There's a rumor that has floated around Okinawa for decades.

They say that in the years after World War II, people fried *sātā andāgī* — Okinawa's beloved round doughnuts — in **waste oil from American military jets**.

I decided to ask the people who were actually there.

---

## The Conversation

**Me 🤓:** "I've heard that people used to fry sātā andāgī in jet engine waste oil after the war. Is that true?"

**A 90-year-old man 👴:** "It wasn't jet engine oil exactly, but there was oil that tasted terrible."

**An 80-year-old man 👨:** "Number 30!"

**Me 🤓:** "Number 30?"

---

## What Is "Number 30"?

That question sent me digging.

"Number 30" refers to **SAE 30**, a grade of motor oil — the kind used in engines, not jet turbines. In postwar Okinawa, which was under U.S. military administration from 1945 to 1972, American military bases were everywhere. And with them came barrels of surplus machine oil.

Food was scarce. Cooking oil was precious. People improvised.

The U.S. military used certain grades of mineral oil that, in small quantities, were not immediately harmful to consume. In the chaos of postwar poverty, some Okinawans obtained these oils — likely discarded or traded informally from the bases — and used them for frying.

The result? *Sātā andāgī* that was edible, but tasted "terrible."

---

## A Doughnut That Carries History

*Sātā andāgī* (サーターアンダギー) literally means "deep-fried sugar" in the Okinawan language, *Uchinaaguchi*. Golden-brown, dense, and slightly crispy on the outside — they are the most iconic Okinawan snack, sold at every market and festival.

Today, they are made with eggs, flour, and sugar, fried in clean vegetable oil. They taste like a warm, doughy memory of something safe.

But behind that sweetness is a longer story.

Okinawa suffered one of the bloodiest land battles of the Pacific War in 1945. When the fighting ended, a quarter of the civilian population was dead. The survivors emerged into a landscape of rubble, under foreign military rule, with almost nothing.

In that world, people found ways to keep living. They fried doughnuts in motor oil. They fed their children. They rebuilt.

---

## Why This Story Matters

When I sat with these two men — one 90, one 80 — and asked about wartime food, they didn't flinch. They remembered specific details. "Number 30." The exact grade.

That precision is the gift of lived experience.

There are fewer and fewer people who carry these memories. Within a decade or two, this kind of firsthand knowledge will be gone. What remains will be what we recorded — and what we chose to pass on.

At **Ryukyu no Tane**, we call this *keiken-chi commons* (経験知コモンズ) — a commons of lived wisdom. The idea that individual experience, when shared and recorded, becomes part of the community's collective intelligence.

A doughnut fried in engine oil. An old man who remembers the exact grade.

This is what we are trying to preserve.

## Sātā Andāgī Recipe (The Modern Version)

If you want to taste a piece of this history — without the motor oil — here is the classic recipe:

**Ingredients** (makes about 15):
- 200g (1½ cups) all-purpose flour
- 100g (½ cup) sugar
- 2 eggs
- 1 tsp baking powder
- Pinch of salt
- Vegetable oil for frying

**Method:**
1. Mix eggs and sugar until combined.
2. Add flour, baking powder, and salt. Mix into a soft dough.
3. Roll into balls (about the size of a golf ball).
4. Fry in oil at 160°C (320°F) for 6–8 minutes, turning occasionally, until deep golden brown.
5. They crack open slightly as they fry — that's the sign they're done.

Eat warm. Think about the people who came before you.
